Research Lines

LLM-based text-to-CAD program generation with B-Rep grounding

Translating natural language descriptions into executable parametric CAD scripts, often with stepwise reasoning or hierarchical structure alignment to improve geometric accuracy and structural consistency.

Open edge: Robustness to ambiguous or underspecified prompts, generalization beyond curated training distributions, and whether the B-Rep grounding strategy limits applicability to other CAD formats or operations.
Multi-modal reconstruction and generation for CAD (mesh/image/point cloud to program)

Recovering editable parametric CAD construction sequences from raw 3D or 2D observations, bridging between perception and editable design representations.

Open edge: Hybrid optimization-based methods may not scale to highly complex freeform geometries; end-to-end models show brittleness under modality shift, and the contribution of the optimization versus the learned prior is unclear.
Unified multi-modal benchmarks and evaluation protocols for AI-assisted CAD

Providing standardized testbeds and metrics that cover geometric fidelity, executability, and engineering intents (manufacturability, functionality, assemblability) across diverse input modalities.

Open edge: Current benchmarks reveal a failure cascade from executable code to engineering-ready design; VLM-based judges introduce their own biases, and benchmark coverage of real-world manufacturing constraints is still limited.
State-space and sequence modeling architectures for long CAD sequences

Handling longer parametric CAD sequences that exceed the context limitations of standard transformers, enabling generation of more complex designs.

Open edge: Evaluation is restricted to specific datasets; the trade-off between sequence length, geometric complexity, and generation validity is not systematically ablated, and transfer to other CAD representations is untested.

Shared Direction

  • Executable program representations (e.g., CadQuery scripts) are the preferred output format, as they guarantee geometric validity and enable downstream editing.
  • Multi-modal inputs (text, images, meshes, point clouds) are being integrated into unified frameworks to lower the skill barrier and leverage diverse data sources.
  • Evaluation is moving beyond geometric similarity (Chamfer distance, IoU) to include engineering criteria such as manufacturability, functionality, and assemblability.
  • Pre-training or fine-tuning on large CAD-specific corpora, combined with reinforcement learning or structured alignment, consistently improves generation quality.

Key Differences

  • Representation granularity: some methods decompose CAD models into hierarchical construction trees with explicit part-level reasoning, while others treat the sequence as a flat token stream or use state-space models for long sequences.
  • Training paradigm: approaches range from supervised fine-tuning plus RL on LLMs, to GAN-based latent space generation, to hybrid optimization that iteratively fits operations with geometric feedback.
  • Evaluation focus: benchmark-driven work emphasizes executability and engineering intent (manufacturability, assemblability), whereas reconstruction-focused work prioritizes volumetric overlap and surface distance.
  • Interaction mode: most methods are single-pass generative models, but one line of work introduces proactive clarification agents that engage in multi-turn dialogue before code generation.

Open Questions

  • Can models trained on clean mesh inputs maintain robustness under real-world sensing noise and modality shifts, as indicated by the brittleness observed in CADBench?
  • What is the relative contribution of the learned generative prior versus the optimization procedure in hybrid mesh-to-CAD methods, and how does this affect scalability to highly complex geometries?
  • Do current benchmarks adequately capture the diversity of real-world engineering constraints, or are they biased toward the specific CAD kernels and operations used in dataset construction?
  • To what extent does the performance gain from agentic clarification depend on the base LLM's reasoning capability, and how would it compare to a simple prompt-engineering baseline?
  • Are the reported improvements in long-sequence CAD generation from state-space models transferable to other CAD representation types (e.g., freeform surfaces) beyond the evaluated parametric sequences?

Research Lines

Benchmark task and dataset design

Creating shared multi-modal evaluation tasks and datasets that make CAD program generation models comparable across input modalities and CAD representations.

Open edge: Task coverage of real-world design workflows, including iterative editing, constraints, and complex surface operations; the included datasets are primarily synthetic and may not reflect production diversity.
Metric and stress-test design

Probing prediction quality, controllability, and robustness beyond aggregate scores through multi-faceted metrics (geometric fidelity, executability, program compactness) and staged evaluation protocols (code, geometry, design-intent alignment).

Open edge: Alignment between these proxy metrics and the engineering decisions users actually care about; metric sensitivity causing model ranking changes under modality shifts; the reliability of VLM-based judges compared to human expert assessment.
Comparable baselines and reporting

Making results auditable via common benchmarks, published datasets, and evaluation scripts, enabling systematic comparison of specialized and general models.

Open edge: Whether benchmark conclusions remain stable across independent re-implementations, different compute budgets, and the inherent variance of LLM-based generation; lack of third-party verification studies.

Shared Direction

  • Multimodal input (clean/noisy mesh, single- and multi-view renders, text) and output (parametric CAD sequences) are necessary to test generalization.
  • Evaluation should go beyond geometric similarity and include executability/validity, program compactness, and some form of design-intent alignment.
  • Model performance degrades significantly under increased geometric complexity and modality shifts, and rankings depend on the chosen metric.
  • Publishing datasets, code, and evaluation protocols is essential for reproducibility, though actual adoption varies.

Key Differences

  • CAD representation: Some benchmarks adopt CadQuery scripts, while others use B-Rep construction trees or neutral parametric sequences.
  • Evaluation depth: Benchmarks differ in whether they stop at geometric validation or proceed to engineering criteria such as manufacturability, functionality, and assemblability.
  • Interaction modes: Most benchmarks evaluate single-shot generation, but one work introduces proactive clarification loops, changing the evaluation setting.
  • Supervision for evaluation: Methods range from geometric optimization (IoU-driven) and rule-based checks to VLM-based rubric judges, with varying degrees of human validation.

Open Questions

  • Do current benchmarks capture the iterative, constraint-driven nature of real CAD design, or do they oversimplify to single-step generation?
  • How well do automated evaluation metrics (e.g., VLM judges) correlate with human engineers' assessments of design intent and usability?
  • Can benchmark rankings be trusted when models are re-implemented by different groups or run under different inference budgets, given the brittleness observed under modality shifts?

Research Lines

End-to-end multimodal CAD generation pipelines

converts text, images, meshes, or point clouds into editable CAD programs (e.g., CadQuery scripts, B‑Rep construction trees, Bézier patches) and achieves high geometric fidelity and executability; examples include systems like FutureCAD, HierCAD, CADFit, DreamCAD, and UniCAD‑MLLM.

Open edge: performance degrades under modality shift and increasing geometric complexity, engineering constraints such as manufacturability and assemblability are not yet integrated into generation, and independent deployment validation is missing.
Interactive clarification and agent-assisted generation

reduces invalid CAD output caused by ambiguous text prompts through a proactive clarifying agent that asks targeted questions before program synthesis, as demonstrated by ProCAD.

Open edge: clarification strategies generalise to complex, multi-step design intents and interactive speed/overhead trade-offs are not characterised; only one exemplar exists in the evidence.
Benchmarks and reproducible evaluation protocols

provides standardised evaluation suites (CADBench, MUSE, UniCAD benchmark) covering geometric fidelity, executability, and engineering properties like manufacturability and functionality, together with datasets and metrics.

Open edge: inconsistency across benchmarks, limited alignment of metrics with real-world CAD engineer workflows, and independent reproduction across teams and infrastructure environments.

Shared Direction

  • All works aim to generate editable CAD models; programmatic representations (B‑Rep or scripts) are preferred to preserve editability.
  • Multi-modal input (text, image, mesh, point cloud) is a shared design choice.
  • Geometric fidelity and executability are universal evaluation dimensions, and code or benchmark releases are a common practice to support reproducibility.

Key Differences

  • Representation: Bézier surface patches versus parametric construction trees versus CadQuery scripts.
  • Supervision: human-annotated CAD datasets versus point-level supervision versus hybrid optimization.
  • Interaction: one-shot generation versus multi-turn proactive clarification.
  • Evaluation focus: geometric metrics (Chamfer distance, IoU) versus engineering-oriented metrics (manufacturability, functionality, assemblability).
  • Architecture: task-specific models versus unified multi-modal large language models.

Open Questions

  • How can generation systems maintain high geometric accuracy under modality shift and high geometric complexity?
  • What is the gap between benchmark metrics and real engineering constraints, and how to bridge it?
  • Can proactive clarification scale to complex, multi-step design tasks without excessive interaction overhead?
  • How do these systems behave when deployed outside the original dataset and hardware environments?

Research Lines

Visual-to-parametric CAD reconstruction

Recovers editable CAD construction sequences from meshes, images, or point clouds, enabling downstream editing and parametric modification.

Open edge: Robustness to real-world images, complex topologies, and unseen construction operations; reliance on training data from specific CAD tools limits cross-tool transfer.
Language/vision-driven CAD code generation

Generates executable CAD code (Python, STEP) from natural language or images, lowering the expertise barrier for creating valid 3D models.

Open edge: Topological integrity of generated B-rep models, generalization beyond the seen code syntax and operation sets, and integration with constraint-based design intent.
Long-sequence parametric CAD generative modeling

Models long sequences of parametric operations to generate complex CAD models with richer construction histories.

Open edge: Scalability to industrial-length sequences while maintaining global geometric consistency and avoiding invalid operations.
Unified multi-modal multi-task CAD frameworks

Handles reconstruction, generation, and question answering across multiple input modalities in a single model, reducing the need for task-specific pipelines.

Open edge: Performance on rare or zero-shot modality combinations, and the trade-off between unified architecture simplicity and per-task optimality.

Shared Direction

  • Editable parametric representations (command sequences, code, STEP) are the central target, not static meshes or point clouds.
  • LLMs and VLMs are becoming the backbone for both generation and reconstruction, replacing earlier geometry-only networks.
  • Geometric fidelity is measured via volumetric IoU, Chamfer distance, and validity ratios; syntactic correctness is also a key metric.
  • Training data remains bottlenecked by the availability of paired geometry-code or geometry-text datasets.

Key Differences

  • Representation: some work uses abstract command sequences, others use human-readable Python code, while yet others target the STEP standard with B-rep topology.
  • Supervision: methods differ in using pure geometric loss versus reinforcement learning with geometric rewards or offline RL to capture sequential decision-making.
  • Generation paradigm: one line of work uses evolution guided by VLMs, another uses fine-tuned generative models or LLMs with retrieval augmentation.
  • Evaluation scope: some papers emphasize low-level geometry accuracy, while others highlight editability, generalization to new operations, or multi-task capability.

Open Questions

  • How well do these methods transfer to real-world photographs or hand-drawn sketches, where view ambiguity and occlusion are present?
  • Can the generated CAD sequences be directly consumed by downstream CAE/CAM pipelines without manual repair?
  • What is the right granularity of representation to balance editability, topological correctness, and model capacity?
  • How can we move beyond single-part generation to assemblies with constraints and functional relationships?

Research Lines

Multimodal benchmarks for programmatic CAD

Provides standardized, execution-verified evaluation suites with diverse modalities, metrics, and design rubrics to systematically measure progress in CAD program generation.

Open edge: Generalization across unseen part families and complex operations is limited; alignment between automated metrics (e.g., Chamfer distance) and engineering requirements (manufacturability, assemblability) is not yet established.
LLM-driven parametric CAD synthesis from text

Leverages large language models to translate natural language descriptions into executable parametric CAD scripts (CadQuery), handling structural reasoning and parameter grounding.

Open edge: Models often hallucinate parametric details, fail on complex geometries like sweeps/lofts, and produce superficially plausible but unfaithful programs; robustness to ambiguous prompts is still a challenge.
Differentiable parametric surface generation

Enables end-to-end training of B-Rep generation from raw point or image supervision without CAD-specific annotations, bridging the gap between editable CAD and unannotated 3D data.

Open edge: The approach depends on the quality of tessellation and may not capture the full topological flexibility of explicit programmatic representations; scaling to industrial-level complexity is unverified.
Proactive clarification and interactive generation

Introduces an agentic loop that audits text prompts, asks targeted clarification questions, and resolves specification ambiguities before code synthesis, improving robustness with low interaction overhead.

Open edge: The interaction cost and the quality of generated clarification questions need further evaluation in real-world design workflows; the approach is currently limited to the text-to-CAD modality.

Shared Direction

  • Parametric CAD representations (B-Reps, CadQuery scripts) are the dominant editable output format because they support downstream engineering workflows.
  • Current LLMs and VLMs can produce plausible coarse geometry but systematically fail to produce faithful, engineering-ready parametric programs, especially for complex operations.
  • Evaluation must move beyond geometric reconstruction to include executability, design intent alignment, manufacturability, and assemblability.
  • There is a clear failure cascade: generating executable code does not guarantee valid geometry, and valid geometry does not guarantee meeting design specifications.

Key Differences

  • Some works advocate for explicit program synthesis with code-generating LLMs, while others argue for differentiable parametric surfaces that can be trained on unannotated data, avoiding the complexity of program generation.
  • Evaluation targets differ: one set of benchmarks prioritizes geometric fidelity and compactness, while newer benchmarks emphasize manufacturing and assembly constraints, leading to potentially conflicting model rankings.
  • Interaction modes vary: one work introduces proactive clarification as a necessary component, while others assume a single-shot generation setting, raising the question of whether clarification should be part of the core generation task.
  • Supervision strategies differ: curated text-to-CadQuery datasets vs. large-scale unannotated 3D data with point-level supervision, leading to trade-offs between precision and scale.

Open Questions

  • Can models trained on existing CAD families generalize to unseen part families and complex parametric operations like sweeps and lofts?
  • How should evaluation metrics be designed to simultaneously balance geometric accuracy, parametric compactness, and engineering criteria such as tolerances and material constraints?
  • What is the reliability of VLM-based judges for design intent alignment, and how do they correlate with human engineering assessments?
  • Is proactive clarification a necessary component for production-grade CAD generation, or can prompt engineering and better training data eliminate the need for interaction?

Taxonomy and roadmap synthesis

Organizes fragmented AI4CAD methods into coherent directions such as generation, reconstruction, and interaction, revealing research gaps and milestones.

Open edge: Whether the taxonomy is backed by systematic evidence across all subfields or relies on a selective sample of recent papers; missing coverage of traditional CAD paradigms.
Comparative evaluation synthesis

Open edge: Results are often not comparable across representations or datasets; no unified benchmark exists for cross-task evaluation, and metric sensitivity to hyperparameters is underexplored.
Open problem and gap mapping

Identifies concrete next questions such as controllable generation, real-world sim-to-real transfer, and long-horizon CAD interaction learning from video.

Open edge: Prioritization of which gaps (e.g., topological guarantees vs. dataset scale) matter most for practical CAD adoption remains unclear.

Shared Direction

  • Deep learning is the primary tool for CAD-related tasks, replacing hand-crafted geometric algorithms.
  • Factorized or multi-stage pipelines are preferred to handle the complexity of CAD structures.
  • Synthetic data plays a crucial role in training due to the scarcity of annotated real CAD data.
  • Code and dataset release is becoming common practice, improving reproducibility.

Key Differences

  • Choice of 3D representation: B-rep offers engineering precision but is harder to generate, while meshes and point clouds are easier but lack topological guarantees.
  • Supervision paradigm: some methods use reinforcement learning for alignment, others use autoregressive or GAN-based generation, leading to different stability and quality trade-offs.
  • Evaluation focus: geometric accuracy vs. topological validity vs. interaction success rate, with no consensus on a primary metric.
  • Interaction modality: some works target automatic generation, others learn from human UI interactions, reflecting different deployment assumptions.

Open Questions

  • How can we design a unified evaluation protocol that fairly compares B-rep, mesh, and point cloud generation methods?
  • What is the minimal real-world supervision needed to bridge the sim-to-real gap in CAD model generation and reconstruction?
  • Can factorized generation approaches guarantee global topological consistency without sacrificing geometric detail?
  • How should long-horizon CAD interaction models be evaluated beyond imitation accuracy, e.g., on task completion or design intent preservation?
